Space Debris

Orbiting high above us, millions of pieces of cosmic debris now threaten the future of space exploration. What began as a modern inconvenience has rapidly escalated into a defining crisis of the 21st century. This growing field of space debris now poses a severe, immediate threat to active global communication networks.

There are many different kinds of space objects. The chart shows a distribution of altitudes of different pieces of space junk. The apogee (highest point in orbit) is on the vertical axis and the perigee (lowest point in orbit) is on the horizontal axis.

The colour represents inclination: high values mean orbits heading towards the poles, and low values mean orbits staying near the equator. The object types are DEB for debris, PAY for payload, and R/B for rocket bodies. Many objects in polar orbit have low altitude, while a lot of them are in low Earth orbit. The poles are very congested, meaning a high risk of collision.

This chart shows how the energy of impact from a piece of space junk in low Earth orbit can be calculated based on how massive the piece is (left axis, in grams) and the angle of impact (right axis, the angle between your orbit and the junk's orbit). Draw a straight line between the two and the impact energy in joules is in the middle. The lines drawn are examples: a fly at orbits 60 degrees to each other would have as much energy as a 9mm bullet, and an AA battery at perpendicular orbits would release as much energy as a stick of dynamite. Since this energy is concentrated on such a small area it causes a lot of damage.

Conclusion

In conclusion, space junk is a significant and growing problem that requires immediate attention and action from the international community. We can mitigate this issue through better space traffic management and the development of technologies for debris removal. You can help by supporting policies and initiatives aimed at reducing space debris as well as engaging in citizen science projects such as Satellite Streak Watcher.
